Manifesto · Terms of Preservation

Doctrine

The Holdings is a private institution for the preservation of owned titles. What follows are the terms by which the Keeper administers the record.

I. On Ownership

A thing owned is a thing held. It does not vanish when a monthly fee goes unpaid. It does not withdraw when a publisher revises its catalog. It does not expire at the end of a quarter. Elsewhere, these conditions are common. They are the defining conditions of rental. Here, they do not apply. The titles admitted to the Holdings are held by the owner of record, and the Holdings does not hold them on any other terms.

II. On the Holdings

The Holdings is not a catalog of purchases. It is a permanent register of titles, each accessioned on the day of its acquisition and preserved without condition thereafter. The register admits no rentals, no leases, no provisional access, no short-term licences. It admits only ownership. A title enters the Holdings once and does not leave.

III. On the Keeper

The Keeper is the custodian of the record. The Keeper accessions, indexes, and preserves. The Keeper does not curate on behalf of the owner. The Keeper does not discourage, recommend, or substitute. What the owner admits, the Keeper records. What the Keeper records, the Keeper defends. The owner is the authority over the Holdings. The Keeper is its memory.

IV. On Perpetuity

Preservation, in the Holdings, is not a slogan. It is the operating condition. A title preserved here is preserved for the working life of the owner's record, with no foreseen end to that record. Servers going dark, services consolidating, catalogs rotating — these occur elsewhere. They do not reach into the Holdings. The rule of the register is simple, and it is the rule by which the Keeper serves: own everything, owe nothing.

— The Keeper
